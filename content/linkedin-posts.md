## Post 1: Contrarian Take — Why Change Programs Fail

70% of change initiatives fail.

But not because the strategy is wrong.

They fail because they're designed by strategists who don't understand behavioral psychology.

As a licensed psychologist who's spent years inside organizations going through major transformations, here's what I see over and over:

1. Leaders assume that once people "understand" the change, they'll adopt it. They won't. Understanding and behavior are different systems.

2. Resistance gets labeled as a problem to overcome. It's not. It's information about what feels threatening — and it's usually right.

3. Change plans are linear. Organizations aren't. You can't Gantt-chart your way through a complex adaptive system.

4. Nobody measures what actually changed in behavior. They measure attendance, satisfaction scores, and "awareness." None of that predicts lasting change.

The 30% that succeed? They treat change the way a psychologist treats behavior change — by understanding what actually drives people.

That's why I built the CLEAR Change Framework.

## Post 2: Framework Teaser — The Step Most Organizations Skip

I built the CLEAR Change Framework after years of watching well-intentioned leaders run into the same wall.

The framework has five phases: Clarity, Leverage, Experimentation, Analysis, Refinement.

But the one most organizations skip? Clarity.

Not "clarity of vision" — every consulting deck has that.

I mean psychological clarity: What are people actually afraid of? Where is trust broken? What are the hidden incentives keeping the status quo in place?

Most change programs start with the solution. CLEAR starts with the real diagnosis.

When you skip that — when you impose a change plan on a system you don't truly understand — you're treating symptoms. And symptoms always come back.

The organizations that get Clarity right don't just implement change faster. They implement the right change.

If your organization is about to embark on a transformation, ask yourself: do we truly understand the psychological landscape we're operating in? Or are we solving the wrong problem really efficiently?

## Post 3: Contrarian Take — Leadership Workshops

Hot take: Most leadership workshops are entertainment, not development.

They change what leaders know. They don't change how leaders lead.

Here's why, from a psychologist's perspective:

Knowledge and behavior are stored in completely different systems. Knowing about emotional intelligence and practicing it when someone is attacking your idea in a meeting — those are fundamentally different capabilities.

A 2-day workshop gives you concepts. It doesn't give you new behavioral patterns.

What does?

→ Psychometric assessment that reveals your actual patterns (not who you think you are)
→ Structured coaching that connects data to specific behavioral goals
→ Applied practice in real situations with feedback loops

That's how adults actually change behavior. Not through slides about "the 5 levels of leadership."

If you've invested in leadership development and nothing changed — you didn't fail. The methodology failed you.

## Post 4: Case Insight (Anonymized)

A mid-size organization came to me because their restructuring was stalling.

Six months in, employee satisfaction was cratering. The leadership team thought it was a communication problem. "We just need to explain the why better."

I ran psychometric assessments and structured listening sessions with teams across the organization.

The problem wasn't communication. It was trust.

Three specific things emerged:

1. Middle managers had been told to "drive the change" but given no authority to adapt it to their context. They felt set up to fail.

2. Two departments had fundamentally different interpretations of what the restructuring meant for their teams. Nobody had surfaced the conflict.

3. The executive team was aligned on the goal but deeply divided on the approach — and that division was visible to everyone below them.

None of this showed up in the engagement survey. All of it was blocking the transformation.

Once we addressed the actual barriers — not the stated ones — movement started within weeks.

The lesson: if your change initiative is stalling, the reason is almost never what people tell you. It's what they don't tell you. That's why you need someone trained to listen.
